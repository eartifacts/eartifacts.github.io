“Monday Morning Quarterbacks” is the penultimate track from Off Minor’s (one of two major bands formed in the wake\nof Saetia’s demise, the other being Hot Cross) debut full-length. It is effectively the ultimate\ntrack, as the following track is a brief solo guitar rendition of Thelonious Monk’s “Off Minor”,\nentirely different from the full-band screamo of the rest\nof the album.

    orchestration

    Also of note is the orchestration here. The ensemble itself is typical of rock\nmusic in general: the classic power\ntrio. But the way that the power\ntrio is used here is unusual (although characteristic of\nOff Minor as a band). The bass is here at the forefront of\nthe arrangement alongside the guitar, and the two weave together their own\nmelodies while the drums play a groove that both reinforces the rhythms of the\nbass and guitar (with the more-or-less \n 6\n
    \n 8\n     feel in the\nsnare and kick drum) and also plays against it (with the straight quarter notes\nin the hi-hats). This particular theme as it is played here is vaguely a\ncall-and-response, with the bass going into its very high register (hence the\nuse of 8va in the transcription) to respond to\nthe chord-melody of the guitar.

    harmony (and melody)

    What follows is a reduction of the theme as transcribed above:

    The first harmonic region here is pretty obviously some kind of A minor chord;\nhere it is labelled as A−7 because\nthe G♮ is played prominently in the bass guitar part.

    \n

    The second harmonic region is a little bit trickier, as the only obvious part\nis that B♭ is in the bass. Arguably, this simple and traditional kind\nof chord-based analysis fails us here, but we can still get some insight from\nit. One could try to analyze this harmonic region as a G−13⧸B♭, or even as some\nkind of B♭ power chord with an added ♯4 and a 6? But here we\nhave chosen E°⧸B♭, for\ntwo reasons. One is that playing a\nstraight-ahead E° over the\nreduction melody seems to sound the most similar to the original. The other\n— perhaps more important — reason is that it makes this into\nessentially a I – V – I progression. The A−7 functions as a rootless F∆7⧸A chord (or rather,\nrootless F∆9⧸A), the tonic\nchord of F major. The E° is the\nvii° of F major, functioning in the same way\nas a (again, rootless) C7, the V7 of F major.

    \n

    Among many other things, this use of what are (virtually) rootless chords could\nbe a contributing factor to why Off Minor often gets the “jazz\npunk” label attached to them, considering that jazz places such an\nemphasis on the 3rd and 7th factors (as well as\nextensions) of chords that it draws away from the root’s importance.
